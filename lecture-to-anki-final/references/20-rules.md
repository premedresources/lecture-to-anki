# Wozniak's 20 Rules of Formulating Knowledge (Applied to Pre-Med)

Source: Piotr Wozniak, "Effective Learning: Twenty Rules of Formulating Knowledge" (1999, SuperMemo)

Rules are ordered by importance. The first 16 all serve one master principle: **make memories simple**.

---

## The Foundation Rules (Must internalize before writing a single card)

**Rule 1: Do not learn if you do not understand.**
Cards built on misunderstood material are worse than useless — they encode the wrong model. If the source material contains a concept the card author doesn't understand, do not card it. Flag it instead. For pre-med: don't card a mechanism you can't explain in plain language.

**Rule 2: Learn before you memorize.**
Flashcards are for *retention* of understood knowledge, not first-pass acquisition. The student should have read the lecture or chapter before cards are used. Cards that short-circuit comprehension create fragile, pattern-matched knowledge that fails under novel question formats (e.g., clinical vignettes).

**Rule 3: Build upon the basics.**
Do not skip foundational cards because they feel obvious. Basic cards are cheap to maintain (short review time) and provide the scaffolding that makes advanced material stick. In practice, ~3-5% of cards consume ~50% of review time — and those are almost always complex cards that could have been decomposed into basics.

---

## Structural Rules (How to shape each card)

**Rule 4: Minimum information principle.**
One card = one retrievable fact. If a card can be answered correctly while still not knowing something important about the topic, split it. Complex answers mean multiple independent memory traces bundled into one card — failure of any sub-part marks the whole thing wrong. This creates instability.

**Rule 5: Cloze deletion is easy and effective.**
Cloze cards are cognitively lighter than Q&A and especially good for:
- Definitions (fill in the term or defining phrase)
- Mechanisms with a logical sequence
- Relationships between structures or processes
- Named laws, classifications, and criteria

**Rule 6: Use imagery.**
Images engage different memory systems than verbal encoding. For anatomy, histology, pathology, and biochemical pathways, images dramatically improve retention. Image occlusion (Rule 8) is the image-native equivalent of cloze deletion. Text-only cards of visual content are a missed opportunity.

**Rule 7: Use mnemonic techniques — sparingly.**
Reserve mnemonics for genuinely arbitrary associations (cranial nerve order, amino acid pKa values). Most pre-med content has internal logic that should be leveraged instead. A card built on understanding is more durable than one built on a mnemonic.

**Rule 8: Graphic deletion is as good as cloze deletion.**
For visual subjects, image occlusion cards where one structure is hidden are superior to text descriptions. One image can yield 10–20 discrete cards.

## Anti-Pattern Rules (What to actively avoid)

**Rule 9: Avoid sets.**
A set is an unordered collection. "What are the 5 causes of X?" is a set question. Sets are hard to retain and don't transfer — knowing a list doesn't mean understanding the items.

**Rule 10: Avoid enumerations.**
Enumerations (ordered lists) are only slightly better than sets. If a list must be tested, convert it: card each item independently, use a mnemonic for the order only if needed, or use a cloze sequence where position provides context.

**Rule 11: Combat interference.**
Interference = learning similar items makes them harder to distinguish. This is the #1 cause of forgetting in mature decks. High-interference zones in pre-med:
- Similar drug mechanisms (e.g., beta-1 vs beta-2 effects)
- Adjacent anatomical structures
- Look-alike/sound-alike terms (hyper/hypocalcemia symptoms)
- Enzyme names with similar substrates

Combat by: adding discriminating context to both cards, creating explicit contrast cards ("How does X differ from Y?"), and marking interference when detected during review.

---

## Quality Rules (How to make good cards great)

**Rule 12: Optimize wording.**
Every unnecessary word increases cognitive load. Strip filler. The question should isolate exactly one retrievable fact. The answer should contain only what needs to be retrieved — nothing more.

**Rule 13: Refer to other memories.**
Connect new cards to existing knowledge. In pre-med: link mechanisms to anatomy, link pharmacology to physiology, link pathology to normal function.

**Rule 14: Personalize and provide examples.**
Abstract knowledge encoded with a concrete example is more durable. A card about a mechanism is stronger when it includes the clinical consequence.

**Rule 15: Rely on emotional/vivid states.**
Surprising, counterintuitive, or clinically alarming facts are remembered better. If a fact has a striking clinical implication, make that the frame — not a dry textbook restatement.

**Rule 16: Use context cues to simplify wording.**
A short tag (e.g., "[Krebs cycle]" or "[Cardiac physiology]") can replace several words of question preamble. Context cues also reduce interference between similar cards from different domains.

---

## Collection-Level Rules

**Rule 17: Redundancy does not contradict minimum information.**
Minimum information = one fact per card. Redundancy = multiple cards covering the same fact from different angles. These are compatible. Cloze + Q&A + reverse cards on the same concept build a richer memory trace.

**Rule 18: Provide source.**
Tag cards with their source (lecture, chapter, lab) so the student can return to context when a card fails.

**Rule 19: Date-stamp volatile knowledge.**
Drug dosages, diagnostic criteria, and clinical guidelines change. Cards covering this material should be tagged as potentially time-sensitive.

**Rule 20: Prioritize.**
Not all testable facts are equal. High-yield, high-frequency facts deserve more redundancy. Low-yield trivia should be excluded or tagged as low-priority. A good card set is curated, not exhaustive.
